About this document

A visual language in development.

This document describes a visual language we are developing for brands to communicate the value of their products.

This is not a finished product. It is a plan we are currently working on — a working draft, meant for internal review and conversation.

02 · Working draft
The problem

Most everyday products are invisible.

A person buys a beer, a croissant, or a piece of chocolate and consumes it in minutes — without thinking about the work behind it.

From growing the ingredients to the final product — behind every simple object is a chain of steps that no one sees.

Brands usually try to fix this in two ways — and neither really works.

03 · The chain nobody sees
01
Documentary style
Showing the real factory. Honest, but often boring — the viewer loses interest before the story begins.
02
Stylized ads
Using actors, glossy cinematography and music. Often empty and generic — the viewer never truly feels the value of the work.
Our solution

A world of miniature craftsmen.

We suggest a completely different visual language. Instead of showing real people in a real factory, we create a world of tiny craftsmen — working with real ingredients in a real human world.

The principle is simple: the product stays its real size, but the workers are tiny. This changes how we see things.

A normal potato becomes a giant boulder. One glass of beer becomes a monument that took dozens of little beings months to build.

04 · Real ingredients, tiny workers
Key principle

Change of scale = change of perceived value.

When a viewer sees how much effort dozens of tiny craftsmen put into one glass of beer, that beer transforms. It is no longer an ordinary product — it becomes something precious.

The contrast is everything: a soft, small felt worker straining against a massive, photographically real ingredient. Work becomes monumental. Effort becomes visible.

The viewer stops seeing a product. They start seeing the story behind it.

RULE 01 / 05

Scale.

The figures are miniature — about 2–3 cm tall. Everything else — ingredients, products, environment — stays at real human size.

The tiny worker is the only thing that doesn’t “belong” in this world. And that’s exactly what makes it feel true.

A1 · Tiny worker
A2 · Real size ingredient
RULE 02 / 05

Material.

The characters are made of felt or wool. The ingredients and products are always photographically real.

This contrast between soft felt and the hard, realistic surface of a potato is intentional. Felt immediately suggests handmade — warmth, care, labour, patience.

B1 · Felt texture
B2 · Real surfaces
RULE 03 / 05

Invisibility.

The craftsmen work in the human world, but real humans never see them. A human shoe might walk past. A cat might run by. But humans do not react to the workers.

They only ever see the result of their work.

C1 · Working unseen
C2 · Only the result remains
RULE 04 / 05

Mystery.

We never explain where they came from. They are not fairy-tale creatures. They are not elves. They simply exist and work.

The viewer creates their own answer. That private wondering is half of the magic.

D1 · No backstory
D2 · They simply exist
RULE 05 / 05

One product.

The story is always about making one single item — one glass of beer, one croissant, one chocolate bar.

The contrast between massive effort and a single result is the emotional hook. A whole season of work, and all of it lands in one human hand.

E1 · One final object
E2 · Massive effort
Story structure

Every video follows the same logic.

Four beats, every time. The repetition is not a limitation — it is what makes the world recognizable. Viewers learn the shape and start anticipating each moment.

01
Mystery
The video opens with close-ups of hard work. The viewer sees the focused little craftsmen, but doesn’t yet know what they’re making.
02
Effort
We show the struggle. Farming, transport, processing. Workers fighting giant ingredients in the cold, the rain, or late at night.
03
Reveal
The camera finally zooms out, and the viewer sees what they were building. This is the “Aha!” moment.
04
Departure
A real human hand takes the finished product. The human has no idea who made it. In the shadows, the tiny craftsmen quietly leave. Their work is done.
Examples of use

From seed to final product.

Three products, three stories, three seasons of unseen work — each ending in a single item that passes into a human hand.

08A · Fries
EXAMPLE · FRENCH FRIES
A year of work for a Christmas tree of fries
An old felt craftsman plants a seed in spring, grows a potato through the summer, and pushes a giant potato across the land in autumn. On Christmas Eve, he builds a tiny Christmas tree out of fries.

EXAMPLE · BEER
A whole season for one glass

Technology & production

Handmade, AI-assisted, carefully composed.

We combine three production layers: handmade stop-motion animation for authenticity, AI tools for speed and scale, and classic post-production to bring everything together.

We are also developing an internal AI prompt system — a set of rules and references baked into the generation pipeline. This ensures every generated output follows our visual rules perfectly: the right scale, the right material, the right mood.

The technology stays invisible. The craftsmen stay the story.
